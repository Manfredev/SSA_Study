Topic 6: Hypothesis Testing (t-tests & Wilcoxon)

Why This Matters

Hypothesis testing lets you determine whether observed differences in simulation output are statistically meaningful or just noise. Choosing the wrong test invalidates your conclusions.

Exam Relevance

Welch t-tests and paired t-tests appear on nearly every exam. You must know which test to use when, compute the test statistic, degrees of freedom, and interpret the p-value correctly.

Prerequisite Recap

Normal distribution, sample mean/variance, the t-distribution arises when estimating the mean of a normally distributed population with unknown variance.

Background Knowledge: Distributions & Significance
Normal Distribution & t-Distribution

The normal $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ is the bell curve. When $\sigma$ is unknown and estimated by the sample standard deviation $S$, the standardised statistic follows a t-distribution instead of a normal — it has heavier tails to account for the extra uncertainty.

$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1)$ when $\sigma$ known. But $T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S / \sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}$ when $\sigma$ estimated by $S$.
Degrees of Freedom

Degrees of freedom (d.f.) = number of independent pieces of information minus number of estimated parameters. For a sample of $n$ values with estimated mean, d.f. $= n - 1$. More d.f. → t-distribution approaches the normal.

With $n = 5$ observations: d.f. $= 4$. The t-distribution with 4 d.f. has much heavier tails than the normal. With $n = 100$: d.f. $= 99$ and $t_{99}$ is nearly $N(0,1)$.
Significance Level vs p-Value

The significance level $\alpha$ is chosen before the test (e.g., 0.05). The p-value is computed from the data — it's the probability of seeing a result this extreme under $H_0$. Reject $H_0$ if $p < \alpha$.

$\alpha = 0.05$, computed $p = 0.032$: reject $H_0$ (significant). If $p = 0.087$: fail to reject (not significant at the 5% level).
One-Tailed vs Two-Tailed Tests

Two-tailed: $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$ (could be larger or smaller). One-tailed: $H_1: \mu > \mu_0$ or $H_1: \mu < \mu_0$. One-tailed tests put all $\alpha$ in one tail, making it easier to reject in that direction.

Two-tailed with $\alpha = 0.05$: reject if $|T| > t_{\alpha/2}$. One-tailed ($H_1: \mu > \mu_0$): reject if $T > t_{\alpha}$ — the critical value is smaller since all 5% is in one tail.

Visual Guide: How to Choose and Perform the Right t-Test

Identify the Design
What are you comparing? One sample vs a known value → one-sample t-test. Two groups → go to Step 2. Before/after on the same subjects → paired t-test.
Check for Pairing
Are observations naturally matched (same subject, same random numbers, same conditions)? If yes → paired t-test (compute differences $D_i = X_i - Y_i$, then do a one-sample t-test on $D_i$). If no → Welch t-test.
Pairing reduces variance by removing between-subject variability. Always prefer pairing when possible.
Select the Test
One-sample: $T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{S/\sqrt{n}}$, d.f. $= n-1$. Paired: same formula on differences $\bar{D}$. Welch: $T = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{\sqrt{S_1^2/n_1 + S_2^2/n_2}}$ with Welch d.f. formula.
Compute the Test Statistic
Plug in sample means, standard deviations, and sizes. For Welch, also compute the degrees of freedom using the Satterthwaite formula (round down to integer).
$\nu \approx \frac{(S_1^2/n_1 + S_2^2/n_2)^2}{\frac{(S_1^2/n_1)^2}{n_1-1} + \frac{(S_2^2/n_2)^2}{n_2-1}}$
Compare to Critical Value
Look up $t_{\alpha/2,\,\nu}$ (two-tailed) or $t_{\alpha,\,\nu}$ (one-tailed). If $|T| > t_{\text{crit}}$, reject $H_0$. Report the conclusion in context: "There is (in)sufficient evidence at the $\alpha$ level to conclude…"
Always state your conclusion in the context of the problem, not just "reject" or "fail to reject."

Theory

One-Sample t-test

Tests whether a sample mean $\bar{X}(n)$ differs from a hypothesised value $\mu_0$. FS: Empirical Tests

$$t = \frac{\bar{X}(n) - \mu_0}{SE}, \quad SE = \sqrt{\frac{S^2(n)}{n}}, \quad df = n - 1$$

Reject $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ if $|t| > t_{n-1, 1-\alpha/2}$.

Paired t-test

When two measurements come from the same experimental units (paired observations):

$$D_i = X_{1i} - X_{2i}, \quad \bar{D} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n D_i$$

Apply the one-sample t-test to the differences $D_i$ with $\mu_0 = 0$: FS: Empirical Tests

$$t = \frac{\bar{D}}{S_D / \sqrt{n}}, \quad df = n - 1$$

Welch t-test (Unpaired, Unequal Variances)

The default for comparing two independent samples when you cannot assume equal variances: FS: System Comparison

$$t = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{\sqrt{\dfrac{S_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{S_2^2}{n_2}}}$$

Degrees of freedom (Welch–Satterthwaite approximation):

$$\hat{f} = \frac{\left(\dfrac{S_1^2}{n_1} + \dfrac{S_2^2}{n_2}\right)^2}{\dfrac{\left(S_1^2/n_1\right)^2}{n_1 - 1} + \dfrac{\left(S_2^2/n_2\right)^2}{n_2 - 1}}$$

Round $\hat{f}$ down to nearest integer for the t-table.

Independent Pooled t-test (Equal Variances)

Only use when you can assume equal variances ($S_1^2 \approx S_2^2$): FS: System Comparison

$$t = \frac{\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2}{S_p \sqrt{\dfrac{1}{n_1} + \dfrac{1}{n_2}}}$$ $$S_p = \sqrt{\frac{(n_1 - 1)S_1^2 + (n_2 - 1)S_2^2}{n_1 + n_2 - 2}}, \quad df = n_1 + n_2 - 2$$

Wilcoxon Tests (Non-parametric)

  • Signed-Rank test: Paired non-parametric alternative. Rank the absolute differences $|D_i|$, sum the ranks of positive differences ($W^+$) and negative differences ($W^-$). Compare $\min(W^+, W^-)$ to critical values.
  • Rank-Sum test (Mann–Whitney U): Unpaired non-parametric alternative. Combine and rank all observations, sum ranks per group. Works when normality cannot be assumed and sample sizes are small.

Common Mistakes

  • Using pooled t-test when variances clearly differ — Welch is almost always the safer choice
  • Confusing $p < 0.01$ with a "large effect" — statistical significance $\neq$ practical significance
  • Forgetting to round $\hat{f}$ down in the Welch test
  • Using a paired test on independent samples (or vice versa)

Practice: Welch t-test on Custom Data

Two systems measured independently. System A ($n=8$): $\bar{X}_A = 42.5$, $S_A^2 = 12.3$. System B ($n=12$): $\bar{X}_B = 38.1$, $S_B^2 = 8.7$.

Topic 7: Output Analysis & Confidence Intervals

Why This Matters

A point estimate without a confidence interval is meaningless. CIs quantify uncertainty. Bootstrap methods let you build CIs even when you can't assume normality.

Exam Relevance

Expect to compute t-CIs, percentile bootstrap CIs, and empirical bootstrap CIs. The percentile rank formula with $j = Np + 0.5$ and linear interpolation is used in nearly every bootstrap question.

Prerequisite Recap

The sample mean is a random variable with its own distribution; the CLT says it becomes approximately normal for large $n$.

Background Knowledge: Sampling Distributions & Bootstrap
Standard Error & Sampling Distribution

The sampling distribution of $\bar{X}$ describes how the sample mean varies across different samples. Its spread is the standard error $\text{SE} = \sigma / \sqrt{n}$ (or $S/\sqrt{n}$ when $\sigma$ unknown). Larger $n$ → smaller SE → more precise estimate.

Population $\sigma = 10$, $n = 25$: $\text{SE} = 10/\sqrt{25} = 2$. Doubling to $n = 100$: $\text{SE} = 10/\sqrt{100} = 1$ (halved).
Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Regardless of the population distribution, $\bar{X}$ becomes approximately normal for large $n$: $\bar{X} \approx N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. Rule of thumb: $n \geq 30$ is usually sufficient unless the population is very skewed.

Rolling a die (uniform, not normal): with $n = 50$ rolls, the average $\bar{X}$ is approximately $N(3.5, 35/(12 \cdot 50))$. The CLT lets us build CIs even though individual rolls aren't normal.
Confidence Level Interpretation

A 95% CI does not mean "95% chance the true mean is in this interval." It means: if we repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the constructed intervals would contain $\mu$. Each specific interval either contains $\mu$ or doesn't.

100 researchers each compute a 95% CI: about 95 of those intervals contain $\mu$, and about 5 don't. You can't tell which group yours falls in.
Bootstrap Resampling Concept

When we can't assume normality, the bootstrap approximates the sampling distribution by resampling with replacement from the original data. Each resample has the same size $n$ as the original. Compute the statistic on each resample to build an empirical distribution.

Original data: $\{3, 7, 2, 9, 5\}$. One bootstrap resample: $\{7, 3, 7, 5, 2\}$ (note: 7 appears twice, 9 is missing). Compute $\bar{X}^* = 4.8$. Repeat 1000+ times.

Visual Guide: How to Compute a Bootstrap CI

Resample With Replacement
From the original $n$ data points, draw $N$ bootstrap resamples (e.g., $N = 1000$). Each resample has $n$ values drawn with replacement from the original data.
Compute Statistic Per Resample
For each bootstrap resample $b = 1, \ldots, N$, compute the statistic of interest (e.g., sample mean $\bar{X}^*_b$). This gives you $N$ bootstrap statistics.
Sort the Bootstrap Statistics
Arrange all $N$ bootstrap statistics in ascending order: $\bar{X}^*_{(1)} \leq \bar{X}^*_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq \bar{X}^*_{(N)}$.
Apply the Percentile Rank Formula
For a $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI, find positions $j_{\text{lo}} = Np_{\text{lo}} + 0.5$ and $j_{\text{hi}} = Np_{\text{hi}} + 0.5$ where $p_{\text{lo}} = \alpha/2$ and $p_{\text{hi}} = 1 - \alpha/2$.
$j = Np + 0.5$
Use $j = Np + 0.5$, not $j = Np$ or $j = (N+1)p$. This specific formula is used in the course.
Interpolate for Bounds
If $j$ is not an integer, interpolate: write $j = \lfloor j \rfloor + g$ where $g$ is the fractional part. Then the bound is $\bar{X}^*_{(\lfloor j \rfloor)} + g \cdot (\bar{X}^*_{(\lceil j \rceil)} - \bar{X}^*_{(\lfloor j \rfloor)})$. Report the CI as $[\text{lower}, \text{upper}]$.
If $j$ happens to be an integer, no interpolation needed — just take $\bar{X}^*_{(j)}$ directly.

Theory

t-Confidence Interval

Assumes normally distributed data (or CLT applies): FS: Basic Statistics

$$\bar{X}(n) \pm t_{n-1, 1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE, \quad SE = \sqrt{\frac{S^2(n)}{n}}$$

For a 90% CI, use $1 - \alpha/2 = 0.95$. For 95% CI, use $1 - \alpha/2 = 0.975$. FS: t-Table

Percentile Bootstrap CI

No distributional assumption needed. Given $N$ bootstrap sample means $Y^* = \{Y_1^*, Y_2^*, \ldots, Y_N^*\}$:

  1. Sort the bootstrap means in ascending order
  2. Find the $p_{\alpha/2}$ and $p_{1-\alpha/2}$ percentiles using the rank formula
  3. The CI is $\left[Y^*_{(p_{\alpha/2})},\; Y^*_{(p_{1-\alpha/2})}\right]$

Percentile rank formula (linear interpolation, $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$): FS: Basic Statistics

$$j = Np + 0.5$$

where $p_i = \frac{1}{N}(i - 0.5)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, N$. Interpolate:

$$Y^*_{(j)} = Y^*_{(\lfloor j \rfloor)} + (j \bmod 1) \cdot \left(Y^*_{(\lfloor j \rfloor + 1)} - Y^*_{(\lfloor j \rfloor)}\right)$$

Empirical Bootstrap CI

Uses bootstrap deviations $\delta_i^* = Y_i^* - \bar{X}(n)$ to correct for bias:

  1. Compute deviations: $\delta_i^* = Y_i^* - \bar{X}(n)$ for each bootstrap mean
  2. Sort deviations, find percentiles $\delta^*_{(\alpha/2)}$ and $\delta^*_{(1-\alpha/2)}$
  3. CI: $\left[\bar{X}(n) - \delta^*_{(1-\alpha/2)},\; \bar{X}(n) - \delta^*_{(\alpha/2)}\right]$

Note the flipped subscripts — we subtract the upper percentile deviation for the lower bound.

Batch Means Method

For autocorrelated simulation output (e.g., steady-state simulations):

  1. Discard the initial warm-up period
  2. Divide the remaining output into $k$ large, non-overlapping batches
  3. Compute the mean $\bar{Y}_i$ of each batch
  4. Treat the $k$ batch means as approximately i.i.d. and build a t-CI

Batches must be large enough that autocorrelation between batch means is negligible.

Common Mistakes

  • Confusing percentile and empirical bootstrap CIs — empirical uses deviations with flipped subscripts
  • Forgetting to sort the data before computing percentile ranks
  • Batch means: making batches too small (autocorrelation not broken)
  • Using $z$-values instead of $t$-values when variance is estimated from data

Topic 8: Comparing System Configurations

Why This Matters

Simulation often compares two or more system designs. You need rigorous methods to determine whether observed differences are real and to decide how many additional replications are needed.

Exam Relevance

Welch CIs for differences and the Dudewicz & Dalal (D&D) two-stage procedure for determining required sample sizes are exam staples. The Bonferroni correction appears when comparing $k > 2$ systems.

Prerequisite Recap

When comparing two systems, the key question is whether the difference is real or just noise; paired designs reduce variance by using the same random conditions.

Background Knowledge: Paired Designs & Multiple Comparisons
Paired vs Independent Samples

Independent: each system is run with its own random numbers — no connection between observations. Paired: both systems are run under the same conditions (same random numbers, same scenarios). The difference $D_i = X_i - Y_i$ removes shared variability.

Comparing two queue designs: independent = run 20 reps of each with different seeds. Paired = run 20 reps, each pair using the same seed. Paired design gives smaller variance of $\bar{D}$.
Why Pairing Reduces Variance

$\text{Var}(\bar{X} - \bar{Y}) = \text{Var}(\bar{X}) + \text{Var}(\bar{Y}) - 2\text{Cov}(\bar{X}, \bar{Y})$. With independent samples, $\text{Cov} = 0$. With pairing, $\text{Cov} > 0$ (positive correlation), so the variance decreases. The stronger the correlation, the bigger the reduction.

If $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \text{Var}(\bar{Y}) = \sigma^2$ and $\text{Corr} = 0.8$: independent gives $2\sigma^2$, paired gives $2\sigma^2 - 2(0.8)\sigma^2 = 0.4\sigma^2$ — an 80% reduction!
Multiple Comparisons Problem

When comparing $k > 2$ systems pairwise, you make $\binom{k}{2}$ comparisons. Each at $\alpha = 0.05$ means the overall Type I error rate inflates. With 10 comparisons, the chance of at least one false positive is $1 - 0.95^{10} \approx 0.40$.

Comparing 5 systems: $\binom{5}{2} = 10$ pairwise comparisons. Without correction, there's a 40% chance of at least one false "significant difference."
Bonferroni Correction

To maintain overall error rate $\alpha$, use $\alpha' = \alpha / c$ for each of the $c$ comparisons. This is conservative (each individual CI is wider) but guarantees the family-wise error rate stays below $\alpha$.

5 systems, $\alpha = 0.05$: $c = \binom{5}{2} = 10$. Each comparison uses $\alpha' = 0.05/10 = 0.005$. Critical values are larger, so only truly large differences are flagged.

Visual Guide: How to Apply the D&D Two-Stage Procedure

Run $n_0$ Pilot Replications
For each of the $k$ systems, run $n_0$ initial replications (the "first stage"). Collect sample means $\bar{X}_i(n_0)$ and sample variances $S_i^2(n_0)$ for each system $i$.
$n_0$ is typically given in the problem (e.g., $n_0 = 10$ or $n_0 = 20$).
Compute Sample Means and Variances
For each system $i$, calculate $\bar{X}_i(n_0) = \frac{1}{n_0}\sum_{j=1}^{n_0} X_{ij}$ and $S_i^2(n_0) = \frac{1}{n_0 - 1}\sum_{j=1}^{n_0}(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_i)^2$.
Calculate Required $n_i^*$
Using the D&D formula, determine how many total replications each system needs for the desired half-width $h$:
$n_i^* = \max\left(n_0, \; \left\lceil \left(\frac{t_{\alpha'/2,\,n_0-1} \cdot S_i(n_0)}{h}\right)^2 \right\rceil \right)$
Run Additional Replications If Needed
If $n_i^* > n_0$, run $n_i^* - n_0$ more replications for system $i$. If $n_i^* \leq n_0$, the pilot data is sufficient — no extra runs needed.
Each system may require a different $n_i^*$ since variances differ.
Construct the Final CI
Using all $n_i^*$ observations for each system, compute the grand sample mean $\bar{X}_i(n_i^*)$. The CI for each system mean (or for pairwise differences) now achieves the target half-width $h$ at confidence level $1 - \alpha$.
For pairwise comparisons of $k$ systems, apply Bonferroni: use $\alpha' = \alpha / \binom{k}{2}$.

Theory

Welch CI for Difference of Means

For two independent samples, the $100(1-\alpha)\%$ CI for $\mu_1 - \mu_2$: FS: System Comparison

$$(\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2) \pm t_{\hat{f}, 1-\alpha/2} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{S_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{S_2^2}{n_2}}$$

If the CI contains 0, we cannot detect a significant difference.

Dudewicz & Dalal Two-Stage Procedure

Determines how many replications are needed to detect a difference $d^*$ with probability $P^*$. FS: System Comparison

Stage 1: Run $n_0$ initial replications for each system $i$. Compute $S_i^2(n_0)$.

Compute required replications:

$$N_i = \max\left(n_0 + 1, \;\left\lceil \frac{h_i^2 \cdot S_i^2(n_0)}{(d^*)^2} \right\rceil\right)$$

where $h_i = h(f_i, P^*, k)$ is read from the D&D table with $f_i = n_0 - 1$ and $k$ = number of systems.

Stage 2: Run $N_i - n_0$ additional replications for system $i$.

Compute weighted means:

$$W_{i1} = \frac{n_0}{N_i}\left(1 + \sqrt{1 - \frac{N_i}{n_0}\left(1 - \frac{(d^*)^2}{h_i^2 \cdot S_i^2(n_0)}\right)}\right)$$

$\bar{X}_i^{(W)} = W_{i1}\bar{X}_i^{(1)} + (1 - W_{i1})\bar{X}_i^{(2)}$, where $\bar{X}_i^{(1)}$ and $\bar{X}_i^{(2)}$ are stage 1 and stage 2 means.

Select the system with the best (highest or lowest) weighted mean.

Bonferroni Inequality

When making $k$ simultaneous comparisons, the overall confidence is at least: FS: System Comparison

$$P(\mu_s \in I_s \;\forall\; s = 1, \ldots, k) \geq 1 - \sum_{s=1}^{k} \alpha_s$$

So for overall $\alpha = 0.05$ with $k = 3$ comparisons, use $\alpha_s = 0.05/3 \approx 0.0167$ per comparison.

Common Mistakes

  • If the Welch CI for $\mu_1 - \mu_2$ contains 0, you cannot conclude the systems are equal — you simply fail to detect a difference
  • Forgetting to use Bonferroni correction when comparing $k > 2$ systems pairwise
  • Using $d^*$ in wrong units (e.g., seconds vs hours) in D&D calculations
  • Forgetting that D&D requires $N_i \geq n_0 + 1$ (you always need at least one additional replication)

Topic 9: Variance Reduction & Monte Carlo

Why This Matters

Reducing variance means you need fewer replications to achieve the same precision. These techniques can save enormous computational effort.

Exam Relevance

CRN variance calculations (with and without) are a classic exam question. Control variates and Monte Carlo integration also appear frequently. Know how to analytically compute $\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2)$ for functions of $U$.

Prerequisite Recap

Variance measures spread around the mean; covariance measures how two variables move together; positive covariance means they tend to increase together.

Background Knowledge: Variance, Covariance & Correlation
Variance & Covariance Properties

$\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2 \text{Var}(X)$. For sums: $\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X,Y)$. If $X, Y$ are independent, $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$ and the cross term vanishes.

$\text{Var}(3X + 2) = 9\text{Var}(X)$. If $X, Y$ independent with $\text{Var}(X) = 4, \text{Var}(Y) = 9$: $\text{Var}(X+Y) = 13$.
$\text{Var}(X - Y)$ Expansion

$\text{Var}(X - Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) - 2\text{Cov}(X,Y)$. This is the key formula for CRN: if $\text{Cov}(X,Y) > 0$, then $\text{Var}(X - Y) < \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$. Positive covariance reduces the variance of the difference.

$\text{Var}(X) = \text{Var}(Y) = 10$, $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 7$: Independent: $\text{Var}(X-Y) = 20$. With CRN: $\text{Var}(X-Y) = 20 - 14 = 6$ — a 70% reduction.
Correlation Coefficient

$\rho_{XY} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)\text{Var}(Y)}}$, always in $[-1, 1]$. $\rho = 1$: perfect positive linear relationship. $\rho = 0$: no linear relationship. $\rho = -1$: perfect negative.

If $X = 2U + 1$ and $Y = 3U + 5$ where $U \sim U(0,1)$: both are linear in $U$, so $\rho = 1$. CRN works best when $\rho$ is close to 1.
Expected Value Linearity

$E[aX + bY + c] = aE[X] + bE[Y] + c$. This always holds, even when $X, Y$ are dependent. Combined with the variance formula, it's the foundation for variance reduction analysis.

$E[X - Y] = E[X] - E[Y]$ regardless of dependence. But $\text{Var}(X - Y)$ depends on $\text{Cov}(X,Y)$ — that's where CRN makes a difference.

Visual Guide: How to Apply Common Random Numbers

Identify the Two Systems
Clearly define systems 1 and 2. The goal is to estimate $\mu_1 - \mu_2$ (the difference in performance measures). CRN works when both systems use the same types of random inputs.
Use the Same $U(0,1)$ Stream for Both
On each replication $j$, feed the same uniform random numbers $U_1, U_2, \ldots$ to both systems. Same arrivals, same service times — only the system configuration differs.
This induces positive correlation: when system 1 has a "bad" run (unlucky arrivals), system 2 does too.
Compute Paired Differences
For each replication $j$, compute $D_j = X_{1j} - X_{2j}$. Because the same random numbers were used, $X_{1j}$ and $X_{2j}$ are not independent — they're positively correlated.
$\text{Var}(D_j) = \text{Var}(X_1) + \text{Var}(X_2) - 2\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2)$
Check Positive Correlation Achieved
Verify $\text{Corr}(X_1, X_2) > 0$ from the data. If not, CRN didn't help (rare if systems use the same logic). Compare $\text{Var}(\bar{D})$ with and without CRN to quantify the improvement.
CRN can backfire (increase variance) if correlation is negative — always check!
Build CI on Differences
Treat $D_1, \ldots, D_n$ as a single sample. Build a t-CI for $\mu_D$: $\bar{D} \pm t_{\alpha/2,\,n-1} \cdot \frac{S_D}{\sqrt{n}}$. If the CI excludes 0, the systems are significantly different.
This is just a one-sample t-CI on the differences — the pairing has already been handled.

Theory

Common Random Numbers (CRN)

Use the same random numbers $U_j$ for both systems. This induces positive correlation, reducing variance of the difference: FS: Variance Reduction

$$\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \frac{\text{Var}(X_{1j}) + \text{Var}(X_{2j}) - 2\text{Cov}(X_{1j}, X_{2j})}{n}$$

If $\text{Cov}(X_{1j}, X_{2j}) > 0$, then $\text{Var}(\bar{Z})_{\text{CRN}} < \text{Var}(\bar{Z})_{\text{ind}}$.

CRN works when both systems respond monotonically in the same direction to the random numbers.

Compare with independent sampling (no correlation):

$$\text{Var}(\bar{Z})_{\text{ind}} = \frac{\text{Var}(X_{1j}) + \text{Var}(X_{2j})}{n}$$

Antithetic Variables

If $X_j^{(1)}$ uses $U$, generate $X_j^{(2)}$ using $1-U$. The estimator averages both: FS: Variance Reduction

$$\bar{X}(n) = \frac{1}{2n}\sum_{j=1}^{n}\left(X_j^{(1)} + X_j^{(2)}\right)$$ $$\text{Var}[\bar{X}(n)] = \frac{\text{Var}(X_j^{(1)}) + \text{Var}(X_j^{(2)}) + 2\text{Cov}(X_j^{(1)}, X_j^{(2)})}{4n}$$

Works when $\text{Cov}(X_j^{(1)}, X_j^{(2)}) < 0$ (negative correlation reduces variance).

Control Variates

Use a correlated variable $Y$ with known mean $\mu_Y$ to adjust the estimator: FS: Variance Reduction

$$X_c = X - a(Y - \mu_Y)$$ $$\text{Var}(X_c) = \text{Var}(X) + a^2\text{Var}(Y) - 2a\,\text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

Optimal coefficient:

$$a^* = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\text{Var}(Y)}$$

Maximum variance reduction: $\text{Var}(X_c^*) = \text{Var}(X)(1 - \rho_{XY}^2)$.

Monte Carlo Integration

To estimate $I = \int_a^b g(x)\,dx$, generate $U_1, \ldots, U_n \sim U(0,1)$ and compute: FS: Derivatives Table

$$\hat{I} = (b-a) \cdot \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g\!\left(a + (b-a)U_i\right)$$

For multi-dimensional integrals $\int_{a_1}^{b_1}\!\int_{a_2}^{b_2} g(x,y)\,dx\,dy$:

$$\hat{I} = (b_1 - a_1)(b_2 - a_2) \cdot \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g\!\left(a_1 + (b_1-a_1)U_i,\; a_2 + (b_2-a_2)V_i\right)$$

Common Mistakes

  • CRN only works when both systems respond monotonically in the same direction to the random input
  • Antithetic variables: using $-U$ instead of $1-U$
  • Forgetting the $(b-a)$ scaling factor in Monte Carlo integration
  • Control variates: using $a^*$ formula but forgetting you need $\mu_Y$ to be known

Topic 10: True/False & Conceptual

Why This Matters

True/false questions test deep conceptual understanding. They're easy points if you know the traps, but costly if you don't. Each answer requires a clear argument — the reasoning matters more than the label.

Exam Relevance

Every exam has 2–4 true/false questions. The argument is worth most of the marks. Common trap topics: significance vs effect size, AS-IS simulation, event vs timing routines, t-distribution usage.

Prerequisite Recap

These questions test understanding of subtle distinctions; the reasoning behind your answer matters as much as the answer itself.

Background Knowledge: Key Distinctions & Common Misconceptions
Statistical vs Practical Significance

A result can be statistically significant (p < 0.05) but practically meaningless (the effect is tiny). With large enough $n$, even trivially small differences become "significant." Always ask: "Is the effect large enough to matter?"

"System A is 0.001 seconds faster than System B" with $p = 0.01$. Statistically significant? Yes. Practically meaningful? Probably not. The CI half-width matters more than the p-value.
Type I vs Type II Error

Type I ($\alpha$): false positive — concluding there's a difference when there isn't one. Type II ($\beta$): false negative — missing a real difference. Power $= 1 - \beta$. You control $\alpha$ by choosing the significance level; $\beta$ depends on effect size and $n$.

Trap: "Failing to reject $H_0$ proves $H_0$ is true." Wrong! It only means insufficient evidence. The true state might be a small effect our test lacked power to detect.
AS-IS Validation vs What-If Analysis

AS-IS: validate the model by comparing it to the real system (does the simulation match reality?). What-if: modify parameters to explore hypothetical scenarios (untestable against reality). You can only validate the AS-IS model; what-if results depend on the model being valid.

Trap: "We validated our what-if scenario by comparing to real data." Impossible! The what-if scenario doesn't exist in reality yet. You validate the AS-IS model, then trust the what-if results.
Common Misconception Patterns

Watch for: (1) Confusing the event routine (processes one event) with the timing routine (selects next event); (2) Claiming $U$ and $1-U$ are "different" distributions; (3) Saying CRN "always" reduces variance; (4) Mixing up when to use $t$ vs $z$ (use $t$ when $\sigma$ unknown).

Trap: "The timing routine updates state variables." No — the timing routine only advances the clock and identifies the next event. The event routine does the actual state updates.

Visual Guide: How to Approach True/False Questions

Read the Claim Precisely
Identify the exact claim being made. Watch for absolute words like "always," "never," "must," "cannot" — these are often the trap. A single counterexample makes "always" false.
Don't skim! "The exponential distribution is memoryless" (true) vs "The exponential distribution is the only memoryless distribution" (false — geometric is also memoryless, for discrete).
Identify the Key Concept
What topic does this claim belong to? (CRN, DES components, bootstrap, t-tests, etc.) Recall the precise definitions and conditions for that concept.
Think of a Counterexample
If the claim seems true, try to think of a case where it fails. If you can find one, the claim is false. If you can't, the claim is likely true — but strengthen your argument with the precise reason.
Good counterexamples are simple and concrete: "Consider $n = 1$…" or "If $\text{Cov} < 0$…"
Check Edge Cases
Consider boundary conditions: $n = 1$, $\lambda = 0$, $\alpha = 0$ or $1$, empty queues, deterministic inputs. Many "obvious" claims break at the edges.
Write Your Justification
State your answer (True/False) and give a concise, precise reason. For True: cite the theorem or property. For False: give the counterexample or explain exactly where the reasoning breaks down.
The justification is worth more marks than the True/False answer itself. A correct answer with wrong reasoning may score 0.

Key Conceptual Traps

Interactive: Flashcard Flipper

Click each card to reveal the answer and explanation.

"$p < 0.01$ means there is definitely a large, noticeable difference."
FALSE. Statistical significance ($p < 0.01$) does not imply practical significance. A tiny, negligible difference can be statistically significant if the sample size is large enough. Always consider the effect size.
"To compare a new policy with the current one, you don't need to simulate the current policy since you already know its real performance."
FALSE. You must run an AS-IS simulation of the current system for two reasons: (1) to validate your simulation model against known real-world performance, and (2) to provide a fair comparison baseline using the same model assumptions.
"The event routine determines what the next event is and advances the clock."
FALSE. The timing routine determines the next event and updates the clock. The event routine executes the event by updating state variables, scheduling future events, and collecting statistics.
"We use the t-distribution instead of the normal distribution because data may not be normally distributed."
FALSE. We use the t-distribution because we estimate the variance from the sample ($S^2$ instead of $\sigma^2$). This estimation adds extra uncertainty, which the heavier tails of the t-distribution account for. As $n \to \infty$, $t \to z$. FS: t-Table
"The whiskers of a boxplot extend to the minimum and maximum values."
FALSE (usually). Standard boxplot whiskers extend to the most extreme data point within $1.5 \times IQR$ from the quartiles. Points beyond this are plotted as individual outliers. Only some non-standard boxplots use min/max whiskers.
"Using a paired t-test with common random numbers and inverse transform reduces variance."
TRUE. A paired t-test on differences $D_i = X_{1i} - X_{2i}$ combined with CRN (same random numbers) and inverse transform (to ensure monotonic response) creates positive correlation, which reduces $\text{Var}(D_i)$ and thus improves precision. FS: Variance Reduction
"The standard deviation of a dynamite plunger plot (bar chart with error bars) can be read as the error bar length."
TRUE. In a dynamite plunger plot, the bar height is the mean and the error bar (whisker) represents $\pm$ one standard deviation. So the SD equals the distance from bar top to whisker top.
"We cannot estimate the standard deviation from a boxplot of normally distributed data."
FALSE. For normal data, the median equals the mean, and $Q_3$ corresponds to the $z = 0.6745$ quantile. So $\sigma = (Q_3 - \text{median}) / 0.6745$. You can read $Q_3$ and the median directly from the boxplot.

Argument Checklist for True/False

  • Always state TRUE or FALSE clearly first
  • Give the definition of the concept being tested
  • Explain why the statement is wrong (or right) using that definition
  • If FALSE, state what the correct version would be
  • The argument earns most marks — a bare "TRUE" or "FALSE" with no reasoning earns almost nothing

Worked Examples

Exercise 36: Four True/False Statements

Statement 1: "Welch t-test with $p < 0.01$ means a noticeable fuel consumption difference"

FALSE. The CLT justifies using a t-test on averages, and $p < 0.01$ is statistically significant. However, statistical significance says nothing about effect size. With large samples, even a negligible real difference can produce $p < 0.01$.

Statement 2: "No need to simulate the current inventory policy since you know its real cost"

FALSE. You must run an AS-IS simulation of the current policy to validate your simulation model. If the AS-IS simulation doesn't match known real performance, your model is wrong and comparisons with alternative policies are meaningless.

Statement 3: "The event routine determines the next event to execute"

FALSE. The timing routine scans the future event list, identifies the next event, and advances the simulation clock. The event routine then executes that event by updating state variables and scheduling new events.

Statement 4: "Paired t-test + inverse transform facilitates variance reduction"

TRUE. With a paired t-test, you can use CRN. The inverse transform method ensures that the same $U$ values generate comparable variates in both systems, enabling positive correlation and thus reducing $\text{Var}(D_i)$.
