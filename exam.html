Question 1 — Poisson Processes 16 points

Scenario: A fitness center smoothie bar receives two types of orders. Protein smoothies arrive at rate $\lambda_1 = 6$ per hour (Poisson), and fruit smoothies arrive at rate $\lambda_2 = 4$ per hour (independent Poisson). Each smoothie has a preparation time that is exponentially distributed with mean 3 minutes, followed by a deterministic processing time of 1 minute.

(a) — 8 points

Calculate the utilization factor of the smoothie bar.

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Part (a)

Step 1: Merge the arrival streams

Since both arrival processes are independent Poisson processes, the merged (superposition) process is also Poisson with rate:

$$\lambda = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 6 + 4 = 10 \text{ per hour}$$

Step 2: Compute mean service time

Each smoothie requires:

  • Preparation: $\text{Exp}(\text{mean } 3 \text{ min})$
  • Processing: 1 min (deterministic)

Mean service time:

$$E[S] = 3 + 1 = 4 \text{ min} = \frac{4}{60} = \frac{1}{15} \text{ hr}$$

Step 3: Calculate utilization

$$\rho = \lambda \cdot E[S] = 10 \times \frac{1}{15} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.667$$

Result

The utilization factor is $\rho = \frac{2}{3} \approx 0.667$. Since $\rho < 1$, the system is stable.

(b) — 8 points

A customer arrives at the smoothie bar and sees 2 people in the queue ahead. The drink station next door has 1 person being served with service time $\text{Exp}(\text{rate } 1/2 \text{ per min})$. What is the probability that the smoothie bar was the better choice (i.e., the customer gets served first at the smoothie bar)?

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Part (b)

Setting up the problem

At the smoothie bar, the customer sees 2 people in the queue plus 1 currently being served. The customer must wait for all 3 to finish before they begin service.

  • Each service has Exp(mean 3 min) prep + 1 min deterministic processing.
  • By the memoryless property, the current person's remaining prep time is still $\sim \text{Exp}(\text{rate } 1/3 \text{ per min})$.

At the drink station, there is 1 person being served with remaining time $\sim \text{Exp}(\text{rate } 1/2 \text{ per min})$ (memoryless property).

Simplified approach (exam-level, pure exponential approximation)

If we approximate the total service as purely exponential (ignoring the 1 min deterministic component for tractability), each smoothie service is $\sim \text{Exp}(\text{rate } 1/4 \text{ per min})$ with mean 4 min.

Smoothie bar wait $W_S$: sum of 3 independent $\text{Exp}(1/4)$ random variables (an Erlang(3, 1/4) distribution). Mean = $3 \times 4 = 12$ min.

Drink station wait $W_D$: $\text{Exp}(1/2)$. Mean = 2 min.

Key insight: Competing exponentials

We want $P(W_S < W_D)$, i.e., the probability the Erlang(3, 1/4) finishes before the Exp(1/2).

Using the Erlang CDF approach: $W_S \sim \text{Erlang}(3, 1/4)$, meaning $W_S = T_1 + T_2 + T_3$ where each $T_i \sim \text{Exp}(1/4)$.

We need $P(W_S < W_D)$ where $W_D \sim \text{Exp}(1/2)$. We can condition on $W_D = t$:

$$P(W_S < W_D) = \int_0^\infty P(W_S < t) \cdot f_{W_D}(t)\,dt = \int_0^\infty F_{\text{Erlang}}(t; 3, 1/4) \cdot \frac{1}{2}e^{-t/2}\,dt$$

The Erlang CDF is:

$$F_{\text{Erlang}}(t; 3, 1/4) = 1 - \sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{(t/4)^k}{k!}e^{-t/4}$$

So:

$$P(W_S < W_D) = 1 - \int_0^\infty \left[\sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{(t/4)^k}{k!}e^{-t/4}\right] \frac{1}{2}e^{-t/2}\,dt$$ $$= 1 - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{1}{4^k \cdot k!}\int_0^\infty t^k e^{-3t/4}\,dt$$

Using $\int_0^\infty t^k e^{-at}\,dt = \frac{k!}{a^{k+1}}$ with $a = 3/4$:

$$= 1 - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{1}{4^k \cdot k!} \cdot \frac{k!}{(3/4)^{k+1}} = 1 - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{1}{4^k} \cdot \frac{4^{k+1}}{3^{k+1}}$$ $$= 1 - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{4}{3^{k+1}} = 1 - 2\sum_{k=0}^{2} \frac{1}{3^{k+1}}$$ $$= 1 - 2\left(\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{9} + \frac{1}{27}\right) = 1 - 2 \cdot \frac{9 + 3 + 1}{27} = 1 - \frac{26}{27} = \frac{1}{27}$$

Result (simplified model)

Under the pure exponential approximation, $P(\text{smoothie bar is faster}) = \frac{1}{27} \approx 0.037$.

The smoothie bar is almost certainly the worse choice — only about a 3.7% chance of being served first there.

Complete analysis (with deterministic component)

The exact analysis is more complex because each smoothie service time is $S_i = T_i + 1$ where $T_i \sim \text{Exp}(1/3)$ and the 1 min is deterministic. This means the total smoothie wait is:

$$W_S = (T_1 + 1) + (T_2 + 1) + (T_3 + 1) = T_1 + T_2 + T_3 + 3$$

where $T_1 + T_2 + T_3 \sim \text{Erlang}(3, 1/3)$. So the smoothie wait is at least 3 minutes plus an Erlang-distributed component with mean 9 min, for a total mean of 12 min. The drink station has mean 2 min.

The probability $P(W_S < W_D)$ requires $P(\text{Erlang}(3,1/3) + 3 < \text{Exp}(1/2))$, which involves conditioning on $W_D > 3$ first (probability $e^{-3/2} \approx 0.223$), then evaluating the remaining competing exponentials. This yields an even smaller probability than the simplified model.

Exam tip

In an exam setting, the simplified exponential approach is typically sufficient for full marks. The key insights to demonstrate are: (1) memoryless property application, (2) identifying the competing exponentials structure, and (3) correct Erlang vs. Exp comparison.

Question 2 — True/False Statements 14 points

Statement 1 — (1 + 6 = 7 points)

"In a chi-squared goodness-of-fit test with $k = 8$ bins and $m = 2$ estimated parameters, using $k - 1 = 7$ degrees of freedom instead of $k - m - 1 = 5$ helps avoid Type II error."

True or False? Justify your answer.

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Statement 1

Answer: FALSE

Explanation

When parameters are estimated from the data, the correct degrees of freedom is:

$$df = k - m - 1 = 8 - 2 - 1 = 5$$

not $k - 1 = 7$.

Why using too many df increases Type II error

  1. Using $df = 7$ instead of $df = 5$ means a larger critical value from the $\chi^2$ table (for the same $\alpha$).
  2. A larger critical value makes it harder to reject $H_0$ — you need a more extreme test statistic to exceed the threshold.
  3. This makes the test less powerful, meaning you are more likely to fail to reject $H_0$ when it is actually false.
  4. Failing to reject a false $H_0$ is exactly Type II error.

Bottom line

Using $k - 1$ when you should use $k - m - 1$ makes the test conservative, which increases (not decreases) the probability of Type II error. To avoid Type II error, you want more power, which means using the correct (smaller) $df = 5$.

Statement 2 — (1 + 6 = 7 points)

"A Wilcoxon rank sum test on two independent samples with the same variance and same shape gives $p < 0.01$. Therefore the means of the two populations are very different."

True or False? Justify your answer.

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Statement 2

Answer: FALSE (for multiple reasons)

Reason 1: Wilcoxon tests medians, not means

The Wilcoxon rank sum test tests whether one distribution is stochastically shifted from the other. When the distributions have the same shape and spread, this is equivalent to testing whether the medians differ — not necessarily the means.

For symmetric distributions, mean = median, but for skewed distributions the mean and median can differ substantially.

Reason 2: Statistical significance $\neq$ practical significance

A p-value of $p < 0.01$ measures the probability of observing data this extreme under $H_0$. It says nothing about the magnitude of the difference.

With a very large sample size, even a tiny, practically meaningless difference can produce $p < 0.01$. The statement "very different" is a claim about practical significance, which cannot be inferred from the p-value alone.

Reason 3: P-value does not quantify effect size

The p-value tells us whether the difference is distinguishable from zero given the sample size and variability. It does not tell us how large the difference is. A confidence interval or effect size measure would be needed for that.

For full marks

Identify at least 2 of these 3 issues: (1) the test concerns medians, not means; (2) statistical vs. practical significance; (3) p-values don't measure effect size.

Question 3 — PDF Composition Algorithm 15 points

Consider the following piecewise PDF:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{1}{2(e^2-1)}e^{3-x} & 1 \leq x \leq 3 \\[8pt] \dfrac{2}{\pi(1+(x-3)^2)} & 3 < x \leq 4 \\[6pt] 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Provide the complete composition algorithm for generating random variates from this distribution.

Your working:

Show Worked Solution

Step 1: Verify this is a valid PDF

We must show the total integral equals 1.

Piece 1: $\displaystyle\int_1^3 \frac{1}{2(e^2-1)}e^{3-x}\,dx$

Let $u = 3 - x$, $du = -dx$. When $x = 1$, $u = 2$; when $x = 3$, $u = 0$.

$$= \frac{1}{2(e^2-1)} \int_2^0 e^u(-du) = \frac{1}{2(e^2-1)} \int_0^2 e^u\,du = \frac{1}{2(e^2-1)}\left[e^u\right]_0^2 = \frac{e^2 - 1}{2(e^2-1)} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Piece 2: $\displaystyle\int_3^4 \frac{2}{\pi(1+(x-3)^2)}\,dx$

Let $t = x - 3$, $dt = dx$. When $x = 3$, $t = 0$; when $x = 4$, $t = 1$.

$$= \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^1 \frac{1}{1+t^2}\,dt = \frac{2}{\pi}\left[\arctan(t)\right]_0^1 = \frac{2}{\pi} \cdot \frac{\pi}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Total: $\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$ ✓ Valid PDF.

Step 2: Identify weights and sub-densities

The composition weights are:

$$p_1 = \frac{1}{2}, \qquad p_2 = \frac{1}{2}$$

The sub-densities (each piece divided by its weight to integrate to 1) are:

$$f_1(x) = \frac{f(x)}{p_1} = \frac{e^{3-x}}{e^2-1} \quad \text{on } [1, 3]$$ $$f_2(x) = \frac{f(x)}{p_2} = \frac{4}{\pi(1+(x-3)^2)} \quad \text{on } (3, 4]$$

Step 3: Find CDF and inverse for each sub-density

Sub-density 1:

$$F_1(x) = \int_1^x \frac{e^{3-t}}{e^2-1}\,dt = \frac{1}{e^2-1}\left[-e^{3-t}\right]_1^x = \frac{1}{e^2-1}\left(-e^{3-x} + e^2\right) = \frac{e^2 - e^{3-x}}{e^2 - 1}$$

Set $U = F_1(x)$ and solve for $x$:

$$U = \frac{e^2 - e^{3-x}}{e^2 - 1}$$ $$U(e^2 - 1) = e^2 - e^{3-x}$$ $$e^{3-x} = e^2 - U(e^2 - 1)$$ $$3 - x = \ln\!\left(e^2 - U(e^2 - 1)\right)$$ $$\boxed{x = 3 - \ln\!\left(e^2 - U(e^2 - 1)\right)}$$

Sub-density 2:

$$F_2(x) = \int_3^x \frac{4}{\pi(1+(t-3)^2)}\,dt = \frac{4}{\pi}\left[\arctan(t-3)\right]_3^x = \frac{4}{\pi}\arctan(x-3)$$

Set $U = F_2(x)$ and solve for $x$:

$$U = \frac{4}{\pi}\arctan(x-3)$$ $$\arctan(x-3) = \frac{\pi U}{4}$$ $$\boxed{x = 3 + \tan\!\left(\frac{\pi U}{4}\right)}$$

Step 4: The Composition Algorithm

  1. Generate $U_1 \sim U(0,1)$.
  2. If $U_1 < 0.5$:
    • Generate $U_2 \sim U(0,1)$
    • Return $X = 3 - \ln\!\left(e^2 - U_2(e^2 - 1)\right)$
  3. Else:
    • Generate $U_2 \sim U(0,1)$
    • Return $X = 3 + \tan\!\left(\dfrac{\pi U_2}{4}\right)$

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Part (a)

Answer: FALSE

Analysis of the MATLAB code

The code generates:

  • $X \sim U(2, 5)$ with density $f_X(x) = 1/3$ on $[2, 5]$
  • $Y \sim U(-1, 3)$ with density $f_Y(y) = 1/4$ on $[-1, 3]$

The code computes:

$$H = \frac{24 \cdot e^X \cdot \sin(Y)}{\log(X^2)}$$

In MATLAB, log is the natural logarithm ($\ln$), and $\ln(X^2) = 2\ln(X)$. Therefore:

$$H = \frac{24\, e^X \sin Y}{2\ln X} = \frac{12\, e^X \sin Y}{\ln X}$$

What Monte Carlo estimates

By the Monte Carlo principle, mean(H) estimates $E[H(X,Y)]$:

$$E[H(X,Y)] = \int_2^5 \int_{-1}^3 \frac{12\, e^x \sin y}{\ln x} \cdot \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{4}\,dy\,dx = \int_2^5 \int_{-1}^3 \frac{12\, e^x \sin y}{12\,\ln x}\,dy\,dx$$ $$= \int_2^5 \int_{-1}^3 \frac{e^x \sin y}{\ln x}\,dy\,dx$$

$$I_{\text{claimed}} = \int_2^5 \int_{-1}^3 \frac{2e^x \sin y}{\ln x}\,dy\,dx$$

But the code actually estimates:

$$I_{\text{actual}} = \int_2^5 \int_{-1}^3 \frac{e^x \sin y}{\ln x}\,dy\,dx$$

Conclusion

The statement is FALSE. The estimate is off by a factor of 2. The code estimates $I_{\text{actual}} = I_{\text{claimed}} / 2$.

Test $H_0: X \sim N(2, 9)$ (i.e., $\mu = 2$, $\sigma^2 = 9$, $\sigma = 3$) at $\alpha = 0.05$.

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Part (a)

Key observation

All parameters are given (not estimated from data), so we use the "all parameters known" adjustment: $(\sqrt{n} + 0.12 + 0.11/\sqrt{n})\,D_n$.

$x_{(1)} = -1.234$, $x_{(2)} = 0.875$, $x_{(3)} = 2.106$, $x_{(4)} = 3.452$, $x_{(5)} = 5.789$

Step 2: Standardize each observation

$z_i = (x_i - \mu)/\sigma = (x_i - 2)/3$:

  • $z_2 = (0.875 - 2)/3 = -0.375$

  • $\Phi(-0.375) \approx \Phi(-0.38) \approx 0.3520$
  • $\Phi(0.035) \approx \Phi(0.04) \approx 0.5160$
  • $\Phi(0.484) \approx \Phi(0.48) \approx 0.6844$

$i$ $x_{(i)}$ $z_i$ $\Phi(z_i)$ $i/n$ $D_i^+ = i/n - \Phi$ $(i-1)/n$ $D_i^- = \Phi - (i-1)/n$

Part (c) — Variance Reduction / CRN (7 points)

Given $X_{1j} = (U - 2)^2$ and $X_{2j} = U^2$ where $U \sim U(0,1)$. Compute $\text{Cov}(X_{1j}, X_{2j})$ analytically. Would Common Random Numbers (CRN) be effective?

Your working:

Show Worked Solution — Part (c)

Key identity for $U \sim U(0,1)$

$E[U^k] = \dfrac{1}{k+1}$, so: $E[U] = \frac{1}{2}$, $E[U^2] = \frac{1}{3}$, $E[U^3] = \frac{1}{4}$, $E[U^4] = \frac{1}{5}$

Step 1: Compute $E[X_{1j} \cdot X_{2j}]$

$$X_{1j} \cdot X_{2j} = (U-2)^2 \cdot U^2 = (U^2 - 4U + 4) \cdot U^2 = U^4 - 4U^3 + 4U^2$$ $$E[X_1 X_2] = E[U^4] - 4E[U^3] + 4E[U^2] = \frac{1}{5} - 4 \cdot \frac{1}{4} + 4 \cdot \frac{1}{3}$$ $$= \frac{1}{5} - 1 + \frac{4}{3} = \frac{3}{15} - \frac{15}{15} + \frac{20}{15} = \frac{8}{15}$$

Step 2: Compute $E[X_{1j}]$ and $E[X_{2j}]$

$$E[X_{1j}] = E[(U-2)^2] = E[U^2 - 4U + 4] = \frac{1}{3} - 4 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 4 = \frac{1}{3} - 2 + 4 = \frac{7}{3}$$ $$E[X_{2j}] = E[U^2] = \frac{1}{3}$$

Step 3: Compute covariance

$$\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) = E[X_1 X_2] - E[X_1] \cdot E[X_2] = \frac{8}{15} - \frac{7}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{3}$$ $$= \frac{8}{15} - \frac{7}{9} = \frac{24}{45} - \frac{35}{45} = -\frac{11}{45}$$

Step 4: Assess CRN effectiveness

CRN is NOT effective

$\text{Cov}(X_{1j}, X_{2j}) = -\dfrac{11}{45} < 0$ (negative covariance).

For CRN to reduce the variance of $\bar{Z}(n) = \bar{X}_1(n) - \bar{X}_2(n)$, we need $\text{Cov} > 0$. Since the covariance is negative, CRN would actually increase the variance of $\bar{Z}(n)$.

Independent sampling or antithetic variates would be better alternatives.

Why CRN increases variance here

$\text{Var}(\bar{Z}) = \text{Var}(\bar{X}_1) + \text{Var}(\bar{X}_2) - 2\text{Cov}(\bar{X}_1, \bar{X}_2)$

When $\text{Cov} < 0$, the $-2\text{Cov}$ term becomes positive, adding to the variance instead of reducing it.
